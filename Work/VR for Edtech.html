Beyond Zoom Fatigue: Validating Spatial UX in EdTech

A rigorous, mixed-methods study utilizing SPSS and G*Power to evaluate how immersive 3D environments impact Social Presence, Student Engagement, and Cognitive Load compared to legacy 2D platforms.

01. The Literature Gap

Can a virtual avatar restore the humanity lost in a video call?

The pandemic established Microsoft Teams and Zoom as educational standards. However, to ensure my research was grounded in actual scientific need, I conducted a systematic narrative review of literature published between 2022 and 2024 via ScienceDirect.

The review revealed a significant UX gap: legacy 2D platforms fundamentally lack Social Presence—the psychological feeling of being "with" another person. My thesis sought to prove whether spatial computing environments (VR) could restore this presence without overburdening the user's cognitive capacity.

  • RQ1: What are the user needs and requirements regarding the design of classroom layouts in virtual reality settings?
  • RQ2: How does the virtual room layout quantitatively affect Social Presence in the context of education?
  • RQ3: Does a VR environment significantly improve Student Engagement compared to established 2D platforms?

Academic & Ethical Rigor

Praised by the assessment board for its "multi-method process," this project was conducted over multiple rigorous phases (Proposal, Ethics, Testing, SPSS Analysis) under the supervision of Dr. Ruth Welsh.

02. Experiment Design

To isolate the impact of the spatial layout and control for individual biases regarding technology, I utilized a Within-Subjects Design (N=20). Every participant experienced both the Control and the Variable, allowing for direct, paired statistical comparisons.

Independent Variable (IV)

The Interface Medium

Prototype A (Control)

Microsoft Teams: 2D UI, static video grids, rigid turn-taking via "raise hand" mechanics.

Prototype B (Variable)

FrameVR Classroom: 3D Spatial UI, personalized avatars, and proximity-based spatial audio.

Dependent Variables (DV)

Psychological Metrics

  • Social Presence (NMSS) Utilized the validated Networked Minds Measure. Subscales evaluated included Perceived Affective Understanding (Cronbach's α = .86) and Emotional Interdependence (Cronbach's α = .85).
  • Student Engagement (UES) Tracked deep immersion, focused attention, and temporal dissociation.
  • Cognitive Load Captured raw quantitative data via post-task mental effort scoring.

03. Protocol & Execution

Participants weren't just asked for their opinions in a vacuum; they were required to perform specific, collaborative educational tasks in both environments. This mixed-methods approach ensured I could capture behavioral observations alongside the self-reported survey data.

The Benchmark

Task 1: The 2D Brainstorm

Users collaborated on a problem-solving task via screen-sharing. Observation noted frequent "camera-off" behavior, leading to passive listening and stunted, sequential interaction.

The Spatial Variable

Task 2: The 3D Collaboration

Avatars navigated a virtual room. Users interacted with a spatial whiteboard, physically positioning their avatars based on the speaker, fundamentally changing the social dynamic.

High Significance

Social Presence (NMSS)

The T-Test proved a statistically significant increase in Social Presence for the VR environment. Specifically, the "Co-Presence" metric spiked because spatial audio and avatar movement successfully tricked the brain into feeling shared physical proximity, eliminating the isolation of 2D grids.

Null Hypothesis Retained

Student Engagement (UES)

Contrary to initial expectations, Engagement scores were statistically comparable between both platforms. As shown in the graph above, VR also incurred a slight (+4.2%) "Novelty Tax" in mental effort. VR improves human connection, but it does not automatically make the curriculum itself more engaging.

05. Actionable UX Insights

Data without application is just trivia. As a Product Designer, my goal was to translate these academic findings into concrete, actionable heuristics for designing spatial and collaborative software.

Insight 1: "The Huddle Effect" Outperforms UI Buttons

Qualitative observation revealed that in FrameVR, participants naturally "huddled" around the shared whiteboard. By utilizing proximity-based spatial audio, they managed turn-taking organically—simply by leaning their avatars in or stepping back.

design_services UX Strategy: Don't design a "Raise Hand" UI button if you can design spatial agency. Mimicking physical human affordances drastically reduces the friction of digital collaboration.

Insight 2: The Cognitive Novelty Tax

The slight increase in mental effort (3.70 vs 3.55) highlighted a learning curve for 3D navigation. Users initially spent precious cognitive energy learning *how* to move, rather than focusing solely on the educational content.

UX Strategy: Intuitive onboarding is mandatory in XR. If the interaction model is too novel, it cannibalizes the user's cognitive bandwidth, leading to faster fatigue.

Insight 3: Immersion ≠ Engagement

The UES scores definitively proved that placing a boring task inside a 3D room doesn't magically make it interesting. Spatial computing is a multiplier for presence, not a replacement for fundamental instructional design.

UX Strategy: Technology should serve the content, not mask it. Product teams must nail the core value proposition and pedagogy first, and apply the medium second.

Scientific Integrity & Research Limitations

A rigorous researcher must acknowledge the boundaries of their data. Due to hardware accessibility constraints for the student demographic, testing was conducted using the desktop mode of FrameVR rather than full HMDs (Head-Mounted Displays). This inevitably dampened the potential ceiling for true "immersion" scores.

"While G*Power validated our sample size (N=20) specifically for a within-subjects design, future studies aiming to generalize findings across broader educational demographics should scale to N > 34 to achieve higher statistical power when comparing multiple prototype variations."
